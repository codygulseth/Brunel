# Lesson 1: Project participants and responsibilities

Commercial construction is a team effort, and the exact arrangement varies by delivery method, contract, company, and project. The descriptions below are common patterns, not universal contractual rules.

## Participants

The **owner** funds the project, defines business needs, and makes the owner decisions reserved by the project agreements. Owners often use a representative to coordinate day-to-day work.

**Architects and engineers** translate requirements into coordinated design documents within their licensed disciplines. The architect commonly coordinates the overall building design. Civil, structural, mechanical, electrical, plumbing, fire-protection, controls, and other engineers cover specialist systems. Their duties depend on their agreements and applicable law.

The **general contractor (GC)** plans and coordinates construction, manages field operations and trade contractors, and commonly owns overall construction schedule, safety, quality, cost, and document workflows to the extent stated in its contract.

**Subcontractors** furnish and install defined scopes of work. **Suppliers** manufacture or provide materials and equipment. Long-lead data-center equipment makes timely submittals, approvals, fabrication, and delivery especially important.

The **commissioning agent or authority** plans and witnesses a structured process for verifying that systems perform according to the project requirements. Commissioning does not replace the designer, installer, inspector, or owner's acceptance authority.

An **Authority Having Jurisdiction (AHJ)** is the public agency or official responsible for permits, code review, and inspections in a location. Different agencies may govern building, fire, environmental, or utility matters.

## Common GC roles

- A **Project Manager** usually leads commercial administration, cost, contracts, schedule coordination, and client communication.
- A **Superintendent** usually leads daily site operations, sequencing, logistics, and trade coordination.
- A **Project Engineer** usually manages information flow: RFIs, submittals, drawings, meeting actions, logs, and follow-up. The role connects office and field teams but does not automatically carry design or approval authority.

## Why clarity matters

Unclear ownership causes delayed answers, duplicated work, missed reviews, and decisions made without authority. A responsibility registry routes questions to the recorded organization or person and shows the source of that information. Humans must verify it against current project documents.

Data centers intensify this need because electrical, cooling, controls, life-safety, vendor equipment, utility, and commissioning work are tightly connected. Long-lead procurement and staged energization create many decision handoffs.

## Typical phases

Projects often move through planning, design, permitting, procurement, sitework, foundations, structure, building envelope, MEP rough-in, equipment installation, testing, commissioning, punch list, and turnover. Phases overlap and may be named or sequenced differently. Responsibility can change by phase, so the governing contract and current project plan remain the source of truth.
